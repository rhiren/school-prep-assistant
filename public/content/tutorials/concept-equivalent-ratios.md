# Equivalent Ratios

## 1. What is this concept?

Equivalent ratios are different ratios that show the same comparison. The numbers may look different, but the relationship stays the same.

You can see this in real life with recipes. If a recipe uses 2 cups of rice for 3 cups of water, then 4 cups of rice for 6 cups of water is the same mix.

## 2. What does it mean?

It means two ratios tell the same story.

For example:
"For every 2 red apples, there are 3 green apples."

That means:
"For every 4 red apples, there are 6 green apples"
is also true.

The numbers changed, but the comparison stayed the same.

## 3. Key Ideas

- Order matters. `2:3` is not the same as `3:2`.
- You can compare part-to-part or part-to-whole, but you must be clear about which one you mean.
- Equivalent ratios are made by multiplying or dividing both numbers by the same amount.
- Simplifying a ratio does not change its meaning.

## 4. How to Solve (Step-by-Step)

1. Identify what is being compared.
2. Determine the type of relationship.
   Is it part-to-part or part-to-whole?
3. Set up the ratio in the correct order.
4. Solve by multiplying or dividing both numbers by the same factor.
5. Check your answer.
   Ask: do both ratios still describe the same comparison?

## 5. Why This Works

Equivalent ratios work like a recipe.

If you double every ingredient, the food still tastes the same. If you triple every ingredient, the taste still stays the same.

That is because the relationship between the ingredients does not change. Ratios work the same way.

## 6. Common Mistakes

- Reversing the order.
  If the ratio is red to blue, you cannot switch it to blue to red.

- Changing only one number.
  If one part doubles, the other part must also double.

- Mixing part-to-part and part-to-whole.
  Comparing blue marbles to red marbles is different from comparing blue marbles to all marbles.

- Adding instead of scaling.
  Equivalent ratios come from multiplying or dividing, not usually from adding the same number.

- Forgetting to simplify.
  A ratio may already be equivalent, but simplifying helps you see the match more clearly.

## 7. Examples (CRITICAL)

### Example 1 (easy)

Is `2:3` equivalent to `4:6`?

Step 1: Look at the change.
`2` became `4`

Step 2: Find the factor.
`2 x 2 = 4`

Step 3: Check the second number.
`3 x 2 = 6`

Step 4: Decide.
Both numbers were multiplied by the same factor.

Final answer:
Yes, `2:3` and `4:6` are equivalent ratios.

### Example 2 (medium)

A drink mix uses 3 cups of juice for every 5 cups of water.
If you use 9 cups of juice, how much water do you need?

Step 1: Identify the comparison.
Juice to water = `3:5`

Step 2: See how the juice changed.
`3` became `9`

Step 3: Find the factor.
`9 ÷ 3 = 3`

Step 4: Scale the water by the same factor.
`5 x 3 = 15`

Step 5: Check.
`3:5` and `9:15` both describe the same mix.

Final answer:
`15 cups of water`

### Example 3 (tricky)

A student says `3:4` is equivalent to `6:9` because both numbers got bigger.

What is the mistake?

Step 1: Check the first number.
`3 x 2 = 6`

Step 2: Check the second number.
`4 x 2 = 8`, not `9`

Step 3: Explain the trap.
Both numbers got bigger, but they did not grow by the same factor.

Final answer:
The mistake is that only one part doubled correctly. `3:4` is not equivalent to `6:9`.

## 8. Real-Life Connection

Shopping can use equivalent ratios too.

If 2 notebooks cost $6, then 4 notebooks should cost $12 if the price stays the same. You are using an equivalent ratio to predict the new cost.
