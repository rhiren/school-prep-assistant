# Percent Relationships

## 1. What is this concept?

Percent relationships help you compare a part to a whole. A percent tells how much out of 100.

You see percents in real life when you look at discounts, test scores, battery levels, and sports statistics. For example, a sign that says "25% off" is using a percent relationship.

## 2. What does it mean?

A percent means "out of 100."

So:
- `25%` means `25 out of 100`
- `50%` means `50 out of 100`
- `100%` means the whole amount

If 30% of a class likes soccer, that means 30 out of every 100 students would like soccer. If the class is smaller than 100, the same idea still works. The percent tells the same kind of comparison.

## 3. Key Ideas

- Percent compares a part to a whole.
- The whole is the full amount, or `100%`.
- You can write a percent as a fraction out of 100 or as a decimal.
- Make sure you know whether you are finding the part, the whole, or the percent.

## 4. How to Solve (Step-by-Step)

1. Identify what is being compared.
   Find the part and the whole.
2. Determine the type of relationship.
   Are you finding the part, the whole, or the percent?
3. Set up correctly.
   Use a percent, decimal, fraction, or equation that matches the question.
4. Solve.
   Multiply or divide carefully.
5. Check your answer.
   Ask: does the answer make sense? Should it be smaller than the whole, equal to the whole, or greater than the whole?

## 5. Why This Works

A percent is like slicing a whole into 100 equal pieces.

If something is 25%, you are taking 25 of those 100 equal pieces. That is why percents help you compare amounts fairly, even when the totals are different.

Another way to think about it:
A percent is like a scoreboard that always uses 100 as the full score. That makes it easier to compare one result to another.

## 6. Common Mistakes

- Using the percent as a whole number instead of a decimal.
  For example, using `25` instead of `0.25`.

- Mixing up the part and the whole.
  If 12 is 30% of a number, 12 is the part, not the whole.

- Using the new amount instead of the original amount for percent change.
  Percent increase and decrease are based on the original amount.

- Subtracting the percent from the number directly.
  For example, `50 - 20` is not the same as finding `20% of 50`.

- Forgetting that more than 100% is possible.
  For example, `150%` means more than the whole.

## 7. Examples (CRITICAL)

### Example 1 (easy)

What is 25% of 40?

Step 1: Identify the part and the whole.
The whole is 40.
The percent is 25%.

Step 2: Set it up.
`25% = 0.25`

Step 3: Solve.
`0.25 x 40 = 10`

Step 4: Check.
Since 25% is one fourth, and one fourth of 40 is 10, the answer makes sense.

Final answer:
`10`

### Example 2 (medium)

A shirt costs $18 after a 10% discount. What was the original price?

Step 1: Think about what 10% off means.
If the price was reduced by 10%, then 90% of the original price is left.

Step 2: Set it up.
`18` is `90%` of the original price.

Step 3: Solve.
`90% = 0.9`
`18 ÷ 0.9 = 20`

Step 4: Check.
10% of 20 is 2.
20 minus 2 equals 18.

Final answer:
`20 dollars`

### Example 3 (tricky)

A student says:
"20% of 60 is 12, so 12% of 60 must be 20."

What is the mistake?

Step 1: Look at the first statement.
`20% of 60 = 0.2 x 60 = 12`
That part is correct.

Step 2: Look at the second statement.
`12% of 60 = 0.12 x 60 = 7.2`

Step 3: Explain the trap.
The student switched the percent and the answer.
Percent relationships do not work backward unless you solve them again.

Final answer:
The mistake is swapping the percent and the part. `12% of 60` is not `20`.

## 8. Real-Life Connection

Shopping uses percent relationships all the time.

If a jacket costs $80 and is 25% off, the discount is one fourth of the price. That means you save $20. Percents help you decide if a sale is really a good deal.
