# Ratios

## 1. What is this concept?

A ratio compares two amounts. It tells how much of one thing there is compared with another thing.

You can write a ratio in different ways, like `2 to 3`, `2:3`, or `2/3`. Ratios are useful for recipes, teams, colors in a design, and many other real-life situations.

What does a ratio mean? It tells the comparison between two groups. If a recipe uses 2 cups of rice for 3 cups of water, the ratio `2:3` means for every 2 cups of rice, there are 3 cups of water.

## 2. Key Idea

- A ratio compares values in a specific order, so `2:3` is not the same as `3:2`.
- Make sure you know what is being compared: part to part or part to whole.
- Ratios can be simplified, just like fractions, if both numbers can be divided by the same number.

Simple picture idea:
Think of a ratio like matching groups in two rows.
`OO` to `XXX` means `2:3`.
If each row doubles, `OOOO` to `XXXXXX` is still the same ratio.

## 3. How to Solve

1. Read carefully and decide what two things are being compared.
2. Decide what kind of ratio it is: part to part or part to whole.
3. Put the numbers in the correct order.
4. Write the ratio using words, a colon, or a fraction.
5. If needed, simplify the ratio by dividing both numbers by the same factor.
6. Check that your answer still matches the words in the question.

Why this works:
A ratio is about keeping the same comparison between two amounts. Simplifying does not change the comparison. For example, `4:6` and `2:3` mean the same relationship.

## 4. Common Mistakes

- Reversing the order of the ratio. If the question asks for red to blue, do not write blue to red.
- Comparing part to whole when the question asks for part to part.
- Forgetting to simplify when the problem asks for simplest form.
- Adding the two numbers and using the total when the total is not part of the question.

## 5. Examples

### Example 1 (easy)

There are 4 red apples and 6 green apples. What is the ratio of red apples to green apples?

Step 1: Find the two amounts being compared.
Red apples = 4
Green apples = 6

Step 2: Write the ratio in the correct order.
Red to green = `4:6`

Step 3: Simplify if needed.
Both 4 and 6 can be divided by 2.
`4:6 = 2:3`

Answer: `2:3`

### Example 2 (medium)

A class has 8 boys and 12 girls. What is the ratio of boys to all students?

This is a real-life example because teachers often compare one group in a class to the whole class.

Step 1: Find the total number of students.
`8 + 12 = 20`

Step 2: Write the ratio.
Boys to all students = `8:20`

Step 3: Simplify.
Both numbers can be divided by 4.
`8:20 = 2:5`

Answer: `2:5`

### Example 3 (slightly tricky)

A student says the ratio of blue marbles to red marbles is `3:8` because there are 3 blue marbles and 5 red marbles.

What is the mistake?

Step 1: Check the actual amounts.
Blue marbles = 3
Red marbles = 5

Step 2: Write the correct ratio.
Blue to red = `3:5`

Step 3: Explain the trap.
The student used `3:8`, but 8 is the total number of marbles, not the number of red marbles.

Why this trap happens:
It is easy to grab every number you see. But first you must ask, "Am I comparing part to part, or part to whole?"

Answer:
The student mixed up part-to-part with part-to-whole.

## 6. Quick Practice

1. A recipe uses 2 cups of rice and 3 cups of water. What is the ratio of rice to water?
2. A team has 9 sixth graders and 6 seventh graders. What is the ratio of seventh graders to sixth graders in simplest form?
3. A bag has 5 blue beads and 15 beads total. What is the ratio of blue beads to all beads in simplest form?

Answers:
1. `2:3`
2. `2:3`
3. `1:3`
